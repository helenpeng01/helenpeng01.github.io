Mississippi summers feel like Laolao’s soupy rice. A mouthful of zhou is thick, syrupy and inches down the throat so slowly that seconds after swallowing, the starchy flavor swims patiently at the back of the tongue. Mississippi summers are hot, sticky, sweltering like they are in Guongzhou, my grandparents’ home. During the summers there, my grandparents tend to their garden of fruits: dragon fruits, mangosteens, and dates. Here, Chinese gourds hang from the vines that crawl over the fence to their angry neighbors’ white picket fence. New winter-melon leaves peak through the twisted stems. We eat soup in the metal bowls they brought in the carry on cardboard boxes. Cornbread substitutes the bing and black-eyed peas substitute pickled vegetables.
