She didn’t know why her hair was like this. The chocolate tendrils insisted on fraying out—poofing into a mushroom of lightweight springs. The kids at public school insisted on calling her Puff Puff. It wasn’t in any mean, sneering way, but the kids at the Catholic school called her by her name. Margot. But then again, it’d been a year now, and perhaps all of her friends at the Catholic school has turned into snickering teenagers too.
It was her birthday today. Her mom has recently decided to put her family on a diet after Halloween, so a large whole-boiled carrot and configuration of stick pretzels greeted her at the dining table, spelling out thirteen. School went as usual. Her tall, lanky limbs awkwardly dangled between crowds between class transitions in middle school. The principle had announced birthdays on the intercom like she did every day, triggering a half-hearted singing of “Happy Birthday” to her in homeroom. It was bland, but she thought she could feel her classmates’ eyes burn into her back a little more today. Maybe it was a teenage thing.
But now, Margot was in front of her bathroom mirror, staring. Her eyes were big, round, and her eyelashes where invisible. Olive skin was stretched across her face, ignoring the concept of cheekbones and dotted with small blooms of the beginning stages of acne. She’d asked her mom for two things for her birthday: Makeup and a haircut. Her mom had refused the makeup but dropped her off the barber’s after school. Margot had cut off all her hair, yet her locks still managed to curl despite the mere inch that remained springing off her head.
